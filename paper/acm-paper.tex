\begin{abstract}
    Humans have always used their hands to interact with the world around them. With the advancement of technology, hand activity recognition has become a crucial area of research in both academia and industry. This paper presents a comprehensive study on hand pattern activity recognition paired with health monitoring using wearable devices. We explore various machine learning techniques to accurately classify hand activities, monitor health parameters and deduce ptential health issues. Our findings indicate that wearable devices can effectively capture hand activity data, which can be utilized for real-time health monitoring and early detection of health anomalies. The implications of this research extend to improving user experience in human-computer interaction, enhancing health monitoring systems, and contributing to the development of personalized healthcare solutions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
    For centuries, humans have relied on their hands to perform a myriad of tasks, from simple daily activities to complex interactions with technology. The ability to recognize and interpret hand patterns is essential for various applications, including human-computer interaction, rehabilitation, and health monitoring. With the proliferation of wearable devices equipped with advanced sensors, there is a growing interest in leveraging these technologies for health monitoring. Recently, these devices have become more sophisticated, capable of capturing detailed motion data that can be analyzed to identify specific certain activities and monitor health parameters, such as heart rate, muscle activity, and stress levels.

    This has been applied to numerous fields, including physical therapy, sports science, and occupational health, where understanding the nuances of someone's health status can provide valuable insights for personalized care.

    Many a case studies have demonstrated the potential of wearable devices in capturing hand movement data, which can be analyzed to identify specific hand activities and monitor health parameters. With the rising prevalence of chronic diseases and the increasing demand for personalized healthcare, there is a pressing need to develop effective methods for real-time health monitoring using wearable technology.

    By combining hand pattern activity recognition with health monitoring, we can create systems that not only enhance user experience in human-computer interaction but also contribute to early detection of health issues and improved management of chronic conditions, such as arthritis, carpal tunnel syndrome, and repetitive strain injuries, but also mental health conditions like stress, anxiety, Alzheimer's disease, and Parkinson's disease.

    This paper will explore various techniques for hand pattern activity recognition using wearable devices, focusing on their applications in health monitoring, specifically neurodegenerative diseases. We will investigate the effectiveness of different machine learning algorithms in classifying hand activities and monitoring health parameters, as well as the potential implications of these technologies for personalized healthcare solutions. Specifically, the first half will address Parkinson's disease, while the second half will focus on dementia and Alzheimer's disease.

\section{Related Work}

    Digital biomarkers have emerged as a promising avenue for monitoring and managing neurodegenerative diseases. These biomarkers, derived from data collected through wearable sensors, offer a non-invasive means to track disease progression and assess treatment efficacy.

    The continuous monitoring of motor and cognitive functions is particularly relevant for conditions such as Parkinson's disease and dementia, where early detection and intervention can significantly impact patient outcomes. Wearable sensors, such as accelerometers and gyroscopes, have been utilized to capture motor symptoms, including tremors, bradykinesia, and gait abnormalities. They also provide insights on individual activities based on hand pattern recognition. Cognitive functions can be assessed through various digital tasks and assessments administered via mobile applications or wearable devices.

    Several studies have demonstrated the utility of digital biomarkers in clinical settings. For instance, research has shown that data from wearable sensors can accurately predict functional abilities in individuals with Alzheimer's disease, particularly in early-onset AD cases. Similarly, digital biomarkers have been employed to monitor the progression of Parkinson's disease, enabling clinicians to tailor treatment plans based on real-time data. Pattern recognition via machine learning algorithms has further enhanced the ability to analyze complex datasets, facilitating the identification of subtle changes in motor and cognitive functions, which may not be easily detectable through traditional clinical assessments, as the lack of continuous monitoring can lead to missed opportunities for early intervention.

    Due to the analysis nature of this paper, we build upon the existing body of work by exploring advanced machine learning techniques to improve the accuracy and reliability of digital biomarkers for Parkinson's disease and dementia. Our approach integrates data from wearable sensors, providing a comprehensive view of patient health and enabling more effective monitoring and management of these neurodegenerative conditions.

\section{Parkinson's Disease Monitoring}

    Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by motor symptoms such as tremors, rigidity, bradykinesia, and postural instability due to the loss of dopaminergic neurons. Monitoring these symptoms is crucial for effective disease management and treatment optimization. Traditional clinical assessments often rely on subjective evaluations, which can lead to variability in diagnosis and treatment decisions. In recent years, digital biomarkers derived from wearable have emerged as a promising solution for continuous and objective monitoring of PD symptoms.

    The main used clinical tools to evaluate Parkinson's disease motor symptoms are the Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (MDS-UPDRS) and the Hoehn and Yahr scale. These assessments are usually done during clinical visits but this can be inaccurate due to their subjective nature and infrequent monitoring.  This relies on the clinician’s experience and intuition rather than reflecting the patient’s daily functioning, leading to potential mismanagement of the disease.

    Given the limitations of conventional clinical scales, there is a clear need for monitoring tools that are objective, quantitative, and capable of continuous operation. Wearable sensors offer a promising solution since they are non invasive, low cost, and portable devices. These characteristics enable motor assessments to be performed not only in clinical settings but also remotely in patients' homes, capturing authentically, real world data that better reflect daily functioning. By providing high resolution, longitudinal measurements, wearables can increase sensitivity to subtle symptom changes, support more personalized treatment decisions, and reduce patient burden by minimizing clinic visits.

\subsection{Methods / Sensor Modalities}

    The use of wearable sensors for monitoring Parkinson's disease covers a range of diverse set of sensors, were each sensor have its own operational principles, advantages, and limitations. Comprehending these modalities is essential for selecting the most appropriate tools for specific monitoring needs, beeing this walking, equilibrium, tremor detection, or other motor symptoms.

    \textbf{Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs):} IMUs are among the most commonly used sensors for PD monitoring. Typically, an IMU integrates a triaxial accelerometer, a triaxial gyroscope, and, in some cases, a triaxial magnetometer. These components are often based on microelectromechanical systems (MEMS), resulting in devices that are low cost, compact, and user friendly. IMUs can be worn on various body parts, such as the wrists, ankles, or trunk, to capture movement patterns associated with PD symptoms like tremors, bradykinesia, and gait abnormalities. The adoption of MEMS-based IMUs has revolutionized functional movement analysis, making precise motion tracking widely accessible in both clinical and research settings.

    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.7\columnwidth]{images/sensors/imu_example.png}
        \caption{Internal components of a typical IMU sensor, showing the microcontroller, memory, integrated IMU chip, power switch, and micro-USB port for data transmission and charging \cite{Brognara2019GaitPD}.}
        \label{fig:imu-hardware}
    \end{figure}

    The main functions of each sensor in an IMU are as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Accelerometer:} Measures linear acceleration along three axes (X, Y, Z). Besides that, can also mesure dinamic acceleration (movement) and static acceleration (gravity). It can detect changes in velocity and orientation, making it useful for identifying tremors and bradykinesia.
        \item \textbf{Gyroscope:} Measures angular velocity around three axes (know as roll, pitch, and yaw). It is particularly effective for capturing rotational movements, which are important for assessing balance and postural stability.
        \item \textbf{Magnetometer:} Measures magnetic fields, which can be used to determine orientation relative to the Earth's magnetic field. This is useful for correcting drift in gyroscope data and improving overall motion tracking accuracy.
    \end{itemize}

    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.7\columnwidth]{images/sensors/most_used_sensor_placement.png}
        \caption{Common sensor placement locations for IMU-based Parkinson's disease monitoring, showing the percentage of articles using each body location. The lower back is the most frequently used location (56\%), followed by both ankles (42\%), both feet (30\%), chest (25\%), and both wrists (25\%) \cite{Brognara2019GaitPD}.}
        \label{fig:sensor-placement}
    \end{figure}

    As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:sensor-placement}, sensor placement varies according to target symptoms and clinical objectives. The lower back emerges as the most prevalent location (56\% of studies) due to its effectiveness in capturing comprehensive gait parameters and overall body movement dynamics. This placement provides a centralized perspective on locomotion patterns and postural control. Ankle and foot placements (42\% and 30\% respectively) are particularly valuable for assessing stride patterns, detecting freezing of gait episodes, and quantifying step variability critical indicators of disease progression. Chest-mounted sensors (25\%) offer insights into postural stability and trunk movements, which are essential for evaluating balance disorders. Wrist worn devices (25\%) excel at detecting upper limb tremor and bradykinesia, benefiting from their proximity to the distal extremities where motor symptoms are often most pronounced. The choice of placement location significantly influences both the quality and type of data collected, making it essential to align sensor positioning strategically with specific clinical monitoring goals and the particular motor symptoms of interest.

    \textbf{Smartwatches:} Smartwatches represent a particularly accessible and convenient sensor modality that has gained significant traction in health monitoring. Compared to specialized medical equipment, these devices are low cost, widely available consumer products equipped with triaxial accelerometers and gyroscopes. Their familiarity and intuitive interfaces make them practical options for widespread adoption among patients and caregivers. These devices have the potential to expand access to objective motor assessments, both in clinical settings and for remote home monitoring. They are particularly promising for evaluating upper limb tremor and fine motor control. Raw data extraction is facilitated through specific applications, enabling in depth analysis of movement signals. By continuously collecting data on motor symptoms throughout the day, smartwatches provide valuable insights into symptom fluctuations, medication response patterns, and overall treatment efficacy.

\subsection{Key Studies}

    The application of wearable sensors in Parkinson's disease monitoring has been extensively validated through multiple research studies. A critical analysis of representative works illustrates the evolution of the field and the potential of this technology, exploring a narrative progression: from the identification of fundamental methodological challenges, through the development of advanced analytical solutions, to the validation of accessible tools for widespread clinical implementation. This progression demonstrates how the field has matured from proof-of-concept studies to clinically viable monitoring systems that can be deployed in real world situations.

    \textbf{Gait Evaluation with Inertial Sensors (Brognara et al.) \cite{Brognara2019GaitPD}:} 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Study Objectives:} This systematic review synthesized empirical evidence from the peer reviewed literature published between 2008 and 2018 regarding the use of wearable inertial sensors for the quantitative assessment of spatiotemporal gait parameters in Parkinson's disease patients. The review examined studies conducted in controlled clinical environments to evaluate the reliability, validity, and clinical utility of inertial measurement units for capturing disease specific gait abnormalities.
        
        \item \textbf{Methodology:} The authors conducted a comprehensive bibliographic research, selecting 36 scientific articles that met the inclusion criteria. They analyzed the use of inertial sensors, focusing on the variability of the number and placement of sensors in the body and the most frequently measured spatio temporal gait parameters. The review also discussed the heterogeneity of the protocols and the lack of clinical standardization, identifying the need for future consensus to increase the replicability of the results.
        
        \item \textbf{Main Conclusions:} The study concluded that while wearable inertial sensors represent useful, objective, and cost-effective tools for gait assessment in Parkinson's disease, substantial methodological heterogeneity and lack of standardization exist across studies. The principal sources of heterogeneity identified were: the number of sensors employed per study (ranging widely, with a mean of 3.2 sensors), sensor placement locations on the body (with the lumbar region being the most prevalent, appearing in 55.6\% of studies, followed by ankle and foot locations), and the considerable diversity in spatiotemporal gait parameters selected for measurement across different studies. These findings emphasize the critical need for establishing internationally agreed-upon protocols and standardized guidelines to facilitate cross study comparisons, enhance clinical reproducibility, and accelerate the clinical translation of wearable sensor technology for routine PD monitoring. The heterogeneity highlighted by this systematic review represents both a challenge and an opportunity for future research to converge on best practices for sensor based gait analysis in neurodegenerative disease assessment.
        
        \item \textbf{Clinical and Research Implications:} This work serves as a crucial reality check for the field, demonstrating that without methodological consensus, the vast potential of wearable sensor technology is undermined by the lack of replicability and comparability across studies a fundamental obstacle to advancing the field toward clinical implementation. While the challenge of standardization remains critical, other researchers have simultaneously focused on a parallel problem like how to extract clear, clinically meaningful signals of disease progression from the vast amounts of data that these sensors generate. This complementary research direction addresses the need for advanced analytical methods and machine learning algorithms capable of converting high-dimensional sensor data into interpretable biomarkers that reflect disease status and therapeutic response.
    \end{itemize}

    \textbf{Disease Progression Monitoring with Machine Learning (Sotirakis et al.) \cite{Sotirakis2023MotorProgressionPD}:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Study Focus:} This longitudinal study investigated whether the integration of wearable sensor data with machine learning algorithms could track the progression in Parkinson's disease with greater sensitivity than conventional clinical scales. The research addressed a critical question: can computational methods detect subtle disease progression that remains invisible to traditional clinical assessment tools?
        
        \item \textbf{Methodology:} The study prospectively followed 74 PD patients over an 18-month period with quarterly assessments. Six IMU sensors were strategically positioned to capture gait parameters and postural balance dynamics. The collected sensor data were used to train a Random Forest (RF) machine learning model designed to estimate clinical scores from the MDS-UPDRS-III scale. The model leveraged high dimensional spatiotemporal features extracted from sensor signals to predict disease severity, enabling direct comparison between ML derived estimates and clinician rated assessments.
        
        \item \textbf{Key Findings:} The ML model successfully detected statistically significant motor progression after 15 months of monitoring. In contrast, clinical evaluations based on the MDS-UPDRS-III scale failed to capture any significant changes during the same timeframe. Furthermore, the ML derived estimates exhibited considerably lower variability compared to clinician assigned scores, suggesting improved measurement reliability and reduced inter rater variability. This finding highlights a fundamental advantage of sensor based assessments: the ability to provide consistent, reproducible measurements that are less susceptible to subjective interpretation.
        
        \item \textbf{Research and Clinical Implications:} This study represents a paradigm shift, demonstrating that the synergy between wearable sensors and machine learning transcends mere symptom quantification. It creates a prognostic tool with superior sensitivity to clinical scales, capable of detecting subtle disease progression that would otherwise go unnoticed. This enhanced sensitivity has profound implications for accelerating clinical trials by detecting therapeutic changes more rapidly and reliably, potentially reducing study duration and patient burden. The approach also enables more precise treatment adjustment based on objective progression markers rather than subjective clinical impressions. This work exemplifies how advanced analytics can unlock the full potential of wearable sensor data, transforming it from raw signals into actionable clinical insights that can revolutionize PD management.
    \end{itemize}

    \textbf{Smartwatch Based Tremor Monitoring (Schifino et al.) \cite{Schifino2025SmartwatchTremorPD}:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Study Focus:} This study aimed to develop and validate an algorithm for assessing upper limb tremor in Parkinson's disease patients using a consumer grade smartwatch (Apple Watch Series 3). The research addressed a critical question in the field, such as, can commercially available wearable technology achieve clinical grade precision in tremor quantification, thereby democratizing access to objective motor assessments beyond specialized research laboratories?
        
        \item \textbf{Methodology:} The validation protocol involved simultaneous data collection from both a smartwatch and a commercial IMU in 21 PD patients and 27 healthy controls. The algorithm employed Power Spectral Density (PSD) analysis a signal processing technique that identifies the strength of different frequency components within a signal focusing specifically on the characteristic frequency range of parkinsonian tremor (3.5–7 Hz). This frequency band analysis allowed the algorithm to isolate tremor specific motion patterns from other movement artifacts and background noise in the sensor data.
        
        \item \textbf{Key Findings:} Smartwatch obtained data demonstrated moderate to strong correlation with commercial IMU measurements, validating the technical fidelity of consumer grade sensors for clinical tremor assessment. The algorithm successfully differentiated PD patients from healthy controls with high accuracy. Furthermore, the tremor metrics extracted from smartwatch data correlated significantly with clinician rated tremor scores from the MDS-UPDRS-III scale, establishing clinical validity. These findings confirmed that consumer smartwatches can provide measurement quality comparable to specialized medical equipment for tremor quantification.
        
        \item \textbf{Clinical and Research Implications:} This work demonstrates that clinically rigorous tremor assessment is no longer confined to specialized laboratories equipped with expensive instrumentation. By validating a widely accessible consumer device, the study democratizes motor monitoring and opens pathways for large scale, real world data collection. The implications extend beyond individual patient monitoring: widespread adoption of validated smartwatch based assessments could enable population level studies of tremor patterns, medication response, and symptom fluctuations that were previously logistically and economically unfeasible. This represents a transformative step toward making objective PD monitoring accessible to broader patient populations, regardless of geographic location or socioeconomic status.
    \end{itemize}

    
\subsection{Critique and Limitations}

    Despite the promise of wearable sensor technology, its widespread adoption for Parkinson's disease monitoring still faces substantial barriers. A critical evaluation of these limitations is essential to guide future research and ensure that proposed solutions are robust, reliable, and clinically meaningful.

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Lack of Standardization:} Methodological heterogeneity remains one of the greatest obstacles to comparability and reproducibility across studies \cite{Brognara2019GaitPD}. Variability appears at multiple levels:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Sensor Configuration:} There is no consensus on the optimal number of sensors or their anatomical placement. Studies range from single lumbar units to multi-sensor arrays spanning limbs, trunk, and head.

            \item \textbf{Parameters and Protocols:} A wide array of spatiotemporal gait parameters and test protocols (e.g., walking distance, inclusion of turns) complicates direct comparison of results.
        \end{itemize}
        
        \item \textbf{Data Analysis Challenges:}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{High Dimensionality:} Wearables generate large, high frequency datasets; extracting clinically relevant information demands advanced feature reduction and analysis techniques, often via machine learning.
            
            \item \textbf{Metric Validation:} Many novel digital metrics lack systematic validation against gold standards (e.g., optical motion capture). Heterogeneous sensor configurations further complicate cross study ML model comparisons because different feature sets emerge from different setups.
        \end{itemize}
        
        \item \textbf{Clinical and Practical Limitations:}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Generalizability of Results:} Many studies, including \cite{Schifino2025SmartwatchTremorPD}, focus on mild, on medication groups, leaving uncertainty about performance in more advanced or off medication states where symptoms are more variable.
            
            \item \textbf{Device Variability:} Sensor characteristics (e.g., sensitivity, noise) differ across brands and models of consumer devices, threatening reproducibility when equipment changes.
            
            \item \textbf{Usability:} There is a fundamental trade off between configuration complexity (which can enhance precision and expand measurable parameters) and patient comfort or feasibility. Multi sensor systems may be impractical for autonomous home use or routine clinic workflows.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{itemize}

\section{Alzheimer's Disease and Dementia Monitoring}

    Dementia, particularly Alzheimer's disease, is regarded as one of the most challenging neurodegenerative disorders to monitor and manage, being the leading cause of death in neurodegenerative diseases \cite{dementia}. To address this, clinicians often assess functional abilities through various tests and observations. However, these assessments can be subjective and may not capture the full spectrum of cognitive decline experienced by patients. 
    
    As dementia often starts with subtle cognitive changes, such as memory lapses and difficulty concentrating, early detection is crucial for effective management. Patterns of behaviour, daily routines, and social interactions that an individual engages in can provide valuable insights into their cognitive health, be it through the presence or absence of certain activities.
    
    Traditionally, these evaluations require patients to to travel to clinical settings, which can be burdensome and may not reflect their everyday functioning, as they could, at the time of the checkup, not display any obvious or alarming symptom. Disruptive global events, such as the COVID-19 pandemic, have further highlighted the limitations of in-person assessments, underscoring the need for remote monitoring solutions.

\subsection{Methods}

    As a result, there has been a growing interest in leveraging digital solutions to monitor or test cognitive function remotely. Some solutions opted for online cognitive tests that patients can complete from home, while others have explored the use of wearable sensors to collect data on daily activities and cognitive function.

    By combining affordability and accessability of wearable devices with ease of usage, researchers have been able to gather comprehensive datasets that provide insights into cognitive decline. Machine learning algorithms have been employed to analyze these datasets, enabling the identification of digital biomarkers that can inform clinical decision-making.

\subsection{Key Studies}

This test provided a convenient and accessible means for patients to monitor their cognitive abilities without the need for frequent clinical visits. However, the analysis of these videos often required both manual and automated methods, which could be time-consuming and resource-intensive, and presented challenges in ensuring accuracy and consistency.

    Other papers have explored the use of digital biomarkers for monitoring Alzheimer's disease and dementia, like \cite{hand-motor-function}. By creating a set of hand movement tasks, they were able to collect data on motor function that could be analyzed to identify patterns associated with cognitive decline. The use of machine learning algorithms allowed for the identification of subtle changes in motor function that may not be easily detectable through traditional clinical assessments.

    Based on the reviewed literature, it is evident that digital biomarkers derived from wearable hold significant promise for monitoring Alzheimer's disease and dementia. The integration of these technologies into clinical practice can facilitate early detection, continuous monitoring, and personalized treatment strategies. By leveraging machine learning algorithms to analyze complex datasets, clinicians can gain deeper insights into disease progression and patient behavior.

    Similar to Parkinson's disease, the use of wearable sensors to monitor motor symptoms, such as gait disturbances and tremors, can provide valuable information for managing Alzheimer's disease. Additionally, they can track cognitive function by monitoring certain daily activities and sleep patterns, which are often affected in dementia patients.

    As seen in \cite{sensing-fine-grained-2019}, fine-grained movement data captured by smartwatches can be utilized to assess hand activity patterns, which may correlate with cognitive decline in Alzheimer's disease. The application of machine learning techniques to analyze this data can enhance the accuracy of digital biomarkers, enabling clinicians to identify subtle changes in motor and cognitive functions.

    More so, the continuous monitoring capabilities of wearable sensors can help identify early signs of cognitive impairment, allowing for timely interventions that may slow disease progression. This is particularly crucial in Alzheimer's disease, where early diagnosis and treatment can significantly improve patient outcomes.

    Not only do these technologies offer a non-invasive means of monitoring, but they also provide a more comprehensive view of patient health by capturing data in real-world settings. This holistic approach can lead to more personalized care plans that address the unique needs of each patient.

    With the use of advanced machine learning techniques, it is possible to analyze complex datasets collected from wearable sensors, facilitating the identification of digital biomarkers that can inform clinical decision-making. This integration of technology into the management of Alzheimer's disease and dementia has the potential to revolutionize patient care, leading to improved outcomes and quality of life.

\subsection{Limitations}

    Unfortunately, despite the promising potential of wearable sensors and digital biomarkers for monitoring Alzheimer's disease and dementia, several limitations must be acknowledged.

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Data Quality and Variability:} The quality of data collected from wearable devices can be influenced by various factors, including device placement, user compliance, and environmental conditions. Variability in data quality may affect the reliability of digital biomarkers and their interpretation.

        \item \textbf{Algorithm Generalizability:} Machine learning algorithms developed for specific populations or datasets may not generalize well to diverse patient groups. This limitation highlights the need for extensive validation across different demographics and clinical settings.

        \item \textbf{Privacy and Ethical Concerns:} The collection and analysis of personal health data raise significant privacy and ethical issues. Ensuring data security and obtaining informed consent are critical considerations when implementing wearable technologies in clinical practice.

        \item \textbf{Integration into Clinical Workflows:} The successful adoption of wearable sensors and digital biomarkers requires seamless integration into existing clinical workflows. This includes addressing challenges related to data management, clinician training, and patient engagement.

        \item \textbf{Longitudinal Studies:} Many studies on digital biomarkers for Alzheimer's disease and dementia are cross-sectional in nature. Longitudinal studies are needed to assess the long-term utility of these biomarkers in tracking disease progression and treatment response.
    \end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
    The path forward is clear: the field must resolve the methodological inconsistencies highlighted by \cite{Brognara2019GaitPD}, leverage the analytical sensitivity demonstrated by \cite{Sotirakis2023MotorProgressionPD}, and harness the accessibility of consumer devices validated by \cite{Schifino2025SmartwatchTremorPD}. Only by integrating these three pillars standardization, advanced analytics, and accessibility can wearable technologies genuinely transform clinical practice.

    Similar to pattern recognition via machine learning algorithms enhancing the ability to analyze complex datasets in Parkinson's disease monitoring, the application of these techniques in Alzheimer's disease and dementia monitoring holds significant promise. By leveraging wearable sensors to capture fine-grained movement data, clinicians can gain deeper insights into cognitive decline and daily activity patterns. The integration of advanced machine learning algorithms can facilitate the identification of digital biomarkers that inform clinical decision-making, enabling early detection and personalized treatment strategies. This holistic approach has the potential to revolutionize patient care, leading to improved outcomes and quality of life for individuals affected by these neurodegenerative conditions.
